0-12 Months

Role Models

In the early years of a child’s life parents/carers are the leading role models and first teachers. They are the greatest influence on the most important developing years of a child’s life. For information on speaking multiple languages in your home please visit:

http://raisingchildren.net.au

Back

0-12 Months

Mother’s Voice

Babies learn to recognise their mother’s voice in utero. By birth your baby has a preference for your voice over other sounds. Babies also begin to learn the difference between sounds not long after birth.

Talk to your baby often and from birth. Use their name, talk about whatever you are doing and repeat words many times. Babies need to hear words lots of times before they understand and use them.

Back

0-12 Months

Body language

In the first three months babies communicate by making eye contact, crying, cooing and smiling. Your baby will smile back at you and will learn other ways of communicating and engaging with you.

Between 3 and 6 months babies learn additional ways to communicate with you such as pointing, blowing raspberries and laughing.

Back

0-12 Months

Listening

Babies understand what they hear before learning to speak. Learning to speak happens through listening, making and playing with sounds, and communicating with others.

Back

0-12 Months

Brain Development

Your baby’s brain will develop rapidly in the first five years of life. Babies early experiences are critical for brain development, impacting on their physical and mental health in adult life!

Back

0-12 Months

Gentle Words

Talk to your baby clearly, slowly and using simple words. Use a soothing voice to calm your baby. Your gentle words and tones, along with cuddles, hugs and rocking are a great comfort to your child.

Back

0-12 Months

Television

Although it may be tempting, avoid placing your child in front of the television for extended periods of time. At this age children need as much interaction as possible. Television does not allow for two way conversations.

Back

0-12 Months

Act on Concerns

If you have any concerns about your child’s development talk to your doctor or child health nurse. The following link provides a guide for indications of developmental delay:

http://www.health.qld.gov.au

0-12 Months

Home Environment

The child’s language environment at home helps them to significantly develop their verbal abilities in their early years.

0-12 Months

First Five Years

There is overwhelming evidence that children’s life chances are most heavily underpinned on their development in the first five years of life. The best learning happens in caring relationships.

Back

0-12 Months

Talk, Talk, Talk

Talking with babies helps them develop their speech, and later their spoken and written language skills. Talk to your baby about what you are doing at the particular time and what you are doing next. Make lots of comments so they can, over time, attach meaning to your words with the appropriate object, person or place.

The tone of your voice is as important as the words you say. Connect the tone of your voice with your facial expression. For example, if you are communicating in a happy tone match this with a smile.

Back

0-12 Months

Read Every Day

Literacy is one if not the most important factors determining your child’s early school success. By reading, sharing books and talking together you are helping your child develop these early foundation skills.

Make sure you spend time sharing books together, every day. Try to think of this time as a special time to connect rather than a task you must do. This will support their love of reading and strengthen the special bond between you. Make it fun and give your child the opportunity to choose the books.

Back

0-12 Months

Tummy Time

Tummy time is important for your baby as it helps them strengthen muscles required for sitting, holding up their head, crawling, walking as well as develop the muscles required to produce speech sounds.

Try different activities to make tummy time interesting. You could try a new book in front of your baby with bright colourful pictures. You might like to try a mirror so they can gaze at their reflection.

Back

0-12 Months

Starting Solids

Starting solids is an exciting messy time! Your baby will love eating surrounded by family as they touch, smell and taste a wide range of foods.

To be ready for solids your baby needs to be able to hold their head steady, sit without help and their tongue should not poke out when food is in their mouth. Look for these signs in the coming months.

Back

0-12 Months

Junk Mail

Junk mail catalogues provide great, inexpensive material for sharing and talking together. You can say the name of the items in the photos. The colourful images and photos of babies, young children and families are attractive to young children.

Back

0-12 Months

Animal Discovery

Most babies find animals fascinating. There is so much to talk about: what type of animal it is, what it looks like, is it big/small, what would it eat, where does it live etc.

Finding animals could be as simple as watching the ants crawl on a path, watching the birds outside in the tree or going for a walk. You could also look into visiting a nearby zoo or wildlife park as your baby becomes increasingly interested in the world around them.

Back

0-12 Months

Outdoors

There is so much to see, explore and talk about outdoors. You can go for a picnic in your backyard or enjoy one of your local parks. Talk to your baby about what you see: trees, birds, green grass, play equipment etc. and let him/her experience the feeling of the leaves and wind.

Take your baby for a walk in the pram and discover places that your baby seems to enjoy. Nature offers beautiful free experiences for everyone to enjoy.

Back

0-12 Months

Friends and Family

Babies love to make friends and play! They learn from others. Local playgroups and libraries are good places to meet other babies and parents.

Go for walks and organise social activities with your friends and family. Make every moment count.

Back

0-12 Months

Play time

Play with your baby. Together read books, sing songs and have fun. You do not need toys, just each other. Count your babies toes and play tickling games.

Support your child to investigate and play with every day objects, so they can make connections to and understand the world around them.

Babies engage in exploratory play, by putting everything in their mouth. You might like to give your baby teething toys. Try cooling a ‘gel’ teether by putting it in the fridge to give your baby a different sensory experience.

Back

0-12 Months

Special Time

By cuddling, playing, talking, singing, looking and smiling at your baby, you are building a secure attachment. Secure attachment is so important as it literally shapes and sculpts babies’ brains.

You can connect with your baby by simply making eye contact and sharing special moments together.

Back

0-12 Months

I’m watching you!

You are the best toy your baby can have. Your baby loves watching you and will learn quickly how to get your attention. Watch how much they love your reaction when they start blowing raspberries to you!

Let your baby watch you do your day to day activities and talk to them while you are doing it. For example, move the high chair near you and let your baby watch you while you prepare food or put your baby in the pram and watch you hang out the washing.

They will love being outdoors and find other interesting things to look at outside. Remember, your baby would rather watch you than a television.

Back

0-12 Months

Repetition

Your baby will let you know what their favourite book and song is. They may kick their legs excitedly, smile or make a particular noise. Babies love repetition and hearing their favourite book and songs over and over again.

Favourite books, toys and songs can be a great comfort to babies when they are upset.

Back

0-12 Months

Massage

Look into baby massage as it has benefits for your baby. It is fun, enjoyable and relaxing for you both. Massage can help baby sleep and helps establish a close connection between you.

For more information please see the link below.http://infantmassage.org.au/

Back

0-12 Months

Dads

Dads play such an important role in babies’ lives. Your baby will love hearing the different tones and patterns of talking when they hear parents reading, talking and singing to them.

Many dads like to find an activity or routine that they can share with their baby such as sharing a few books together, enjoying play and bath time.

Back

0-12 Months

Music fun

Music encourages multiple parts of a developing brain to activate. Singing songs requires your child to process the words, rhythm, pattern and melody. These all involve different neurological activities.

In fact, music and movement help children develop ‘cross lateral’ skills (those that involve one part of the brain and then the other and are essential for reading).

Sing often to your baby. Sing new, as well as familiar songs. Play music at home and see how they respond.

Back

0-12 Months

Hide and Seek

Babies love hiding games! Play peek-a-boo games when your baby is young. Babies’ sense of object permanence is vital for understanding that an object still exists when it is out of sight. When he/she is mobile you can play hiding games and pretend that you are trying to find them. Use descriptive language to let your baby know where you are looking and where you are looking next.

Back

0-12 Months

Reading With Your baby

Read to your baby from birth. Choose books with bright, contrasting colours and thick cardboard pages with that will not rip. You do not have to read all the words you can talk about the pictures or make up your own stories.

Try books with interesting pictures, different textures and photos of babies (they love these). Explore the baby books section at your local library.

Back

0-12 Months

Books

When you share books with your baby this helps them to listen to the flow of language, hear patterns and find colours. These help to map early neurological foundations essential for reading and writing. Babies love to look at picture books with photos of themselves, other babies and families.

You can make your own photo book for your baby to look at. Choose a small exercise book and print off pictures of your family, including lots of your baby, pets, extended family and friends. Talk to your baby about the names of the people in the pictures, when the photo was taken and what they like.

Back

0-12 Months

No Cook Recipes

You can involve your baby in the kitchen by trying simple no cook recipes together. You could put a cup in your baby’s hand and help them scoop out flour or oats. You can help them to pour in small amounts of liquid.

They may like to use their own spoon and copy you stirring the mixture. Avoid ingredients that you do not want your baby to put in their mouth (e.g. raw meat, eggs, nuts).

A very simple idea is to make a clown face on a plate of various chopped fruit and vegetables.

You may like to look up ‘no cook’ recipes from the website link below.www.taste.com.au

Back

0-12 Months

Singing

Sing to your baby from birth. Do not worry if you don’t sound great! It is a special way to grow the bond between you. You can make daily routines, such as nappy changing or bath time fun by singing songs. Watch your baby’s face and see his/her reaction. Which one do you think they like the most? Try songs with actions as babies and young children love them.

Action songs help build their co-ordination skills. Some popular ones to try are: Twinkle Twinkle Little Star, Incy Wincy Spider, Open Shut Them and The Wheels on the Bus. Did you know that repeating favourite songs and rhymes will help your baby learn about language and rhythm?

Back

0-12 Months

Nursery rhymes

Have fun with nursery rhymes. Nursery rhymes can help your child to learn new words and sounds, develop their listening skills and build strong foundations for early literacy.

Try nursery rhyme CDs in the car. You can both sing along together. You can borrow CDs from your local library. Forgotten the words to nursery rhymes? Look up the words of your favourite ones from your own childhood. A great resource is the Baby Karaoke app available from the Raising Children Network website:

www.raisingchildren.net.au

For the words to favourite nursery rhymes visit:

www.kidspot.com.au

0-12 Months

Libraries

Libraries have a range of baby and children’s books for you to explore together. See if your local library offers programs especially for babies. As well as offering interesting programs for your baby and a wide selection of books, library membership is usually free!

Library visits are a great routine to start early in your baby’s life and provide a great social experience.

Back

0-12 Months

Playdough discovery

Playdough is so fascinating for babies and young children. Imagine feeling the consistency of playdough for the first time! You can make your own playdough. Here is a no cook recipe for you to try:

http://theimaginationtree.com

Make simple characters, a smiley face, shapes, long strings or bracelets. Talk to your baby about the colour, shape and how it feels (e.g. squishy, squashy, cold, soft etc).

Back

0-12 Months

Messy Play

Let your baby have fun and experiment with the different feelings of squishing finger paint or jelly, the soft feeling of shaving cream, the texture of sand and splashing water around. Always supervise your baby during messy play.

You may like to set up messy play using an old children’s table, using disposable tablecloths or a high chair that can be easily wiped down.

Back

0-12 Months

Textures

Exploring textures is so much fun. Babies love the sensory experience of touching different surfaces and materials. You can easily make your own textured books from every day materials you have around house.

Try to incorporate different surfaces such as smooth, bumpy, glossy, shiny and textures such as cotton wool, felt, tissue paper, silk and cellophane. Talk with your baby about these wonderful sensory experiences.

Back

0-12 Months

Bubbles

Your baby will be fascinated by bubbles. You can buy some bubble mixture and blow bubbles to your baby. Talk to them about the different sized bubbles, the colours that appear and how they move in the wind.

When your baby is able to sit you can let your baby have fun touching bubbles. Always supervise your baby around water and never leave them unattended.

Back

0-12 Months

Sensory Experiences

When your baby is able to sit in the high chair you can introduce him/her to simple painting activities. You can buy or make some finger paint (ensuring it is non-toxic) and let them run their fingers through it on the tray of the high chair.

An easy edible finger paint recipe can be found at:

http://theimaginationtree.com

Back

0-12 Months

Awakening the Senses

Try different sensory activities such as sand play, bubbles, hair rollers, shaving cream or playdough on the tray of the high chair.

Simple activities such as rolling a plastic ball back and forth, investigating how to make it move and checking out the noise and effect of scrunching up paper are new and fun experiences for baby.

Back

0-12 Months

Water Play

Water play is lots of fun. Include different sized cups so your baby can pour water from one point to another. Make it interesting by adding a little bit of food colouring.

You could put different coloured water in a couple of plastic containers so that your baby can experiment with volume and mixing colours.

Talk to your baby about what they are focused on. Always supervise your baby around water and never leave your baby unattended.

Back

0-12 Months

Treasure Time

A simple idea is to make your own treasure basket at home using every day household items e.g. a sock, peg, pencil, spoon, leaf, toothbrush etc. Put 4-5 objects in a basket or shallow container and cover it with a cloth. Encourage your baby to put their hand underneath and see what they can find. Your baby will be so inquisitive!

Make comments about the item they have ‘discovered’. For example, you could comment about how the object feels, how it is used and its colour.

Back

0-12 Months

Musical Instruments

Introduce simple instruments such as shakers and rattles. They can be bought or homemade. Allow your baby to experiment with the different sounds. Creating music together can be a positive and joyful experience enhancing your baby’s emotional well-being.

Making music encourages self-expression and self-confidence particularly for children who are not talking yet. Making music is a fabulous way for your baby to communicate a thought or a feeling.

13 months - 2.5 years

Expanding

Talk to toddlers about what they are doing. If they point to an object, tell them what it is in a sentence (e.g. It’s a yellow banana) not just a single word.

Expand on your child’s own talk such as if they say “dolly drink” you say “Yes, the dolly is drinking the milk”. This is a great role model for language development.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Naming Things

Your child will say nouns (naming words) first e.g. car, ball, doll, dad, mum, dog etc. Name objects and repeat the word so your child can associate the word with the object. A child’s vocabulary is one of the most robust long term predictors of successful literacy development.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

New Words

Some children gain new words rapidly, others take a little longer. As a guide, your child should have approximately 50 words at age 2.

If you are concerned about your child’s speech development consult a speech pathologist or contact your local child health clinic.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Body Parts

Your child will know and point to more body parts than they can say. They will start to say some body parts beginning with obvious ones like nose, eyes, ears, etc.

Expand their knowledge by gradually adding a new or unfamiliar body part e.g. shin, elbow etc.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Take Time

Give your child enough time to respond to your statement or question before adding additional words. It may feel unnatural but if you wait and pause, your child will have the time required to process information and develop a response.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Encouragement

Some children need a lot of encouragement and positive feedback to begin talking. Others will be desperate to talk to you! Either way, try and stop what you are doing and take the time to listen and encourage.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Hearing

If you have any concerns about your baby’s hearing, talk to your doctor or healthcare nurse.

http://www.health.qld.gov.au

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Understanding Words

Using age appropriate language helps young children to understand you best. Always speak clearly to your child using the correct grammar.

Make sure you use language that your child can keep up with. Tune in to your child’s language abilities.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Speak Well

If you consistently use baby talk, be mindful of how much you use. Your child will, in time, need to learn the correct names for objects as others at child care, kindergarten and school will not necessarily understand your terms and this could create confusion for your child.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Making things clear

Children do not always understand everything we say. If your child looks puzzled when you say something, try saying it in a different way or show them what you mean.

Children learn by doing and observing.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Read Every Day

Literacy is one of the most important factors determining your child’s early school success. By reading and sharing books and talking together you are helping your child develop these early foundation skills. Make sure you spend time sharing books together, every day.

Try to think of this time as a special time to connect rather than a task you must do. This will support their love of reading and strengthen the special bond between you both. Make it fun and give your child the opportunity to choose the books.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Say Hello

Greet toddlers by saying hello and using their name. Try to get down to their eye level to talk to them. You can try some ‘introduction songs’ with your child, their friends or just for fun with your family members.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Outdoor Fun

Spend time outside with toddlers talking, listening and exploring. Pot simple plants together or plant seeds and make it a routine to check on the progress of your plants.

You might like to try potting plants with flowers that are purple, yellow or orange as these attract butterflies into your garden.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Photo Albums

Make a photo album for your toddler with photos of your family and pets and friends. Remember to put some photos of them in there too. Repeat the name of the person on each page and add a comment about them.

Store the album on your child’s bookshelf or somewhere at their level so they can easily look at the photo album.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Playgroups

Your little one loves to be social and will delight in doing activities or playing with friends their own age.

If you do not have many contacts (friends/family) with children of a similar age, make time to contact local playgroups and check out whether any local schools have playgroups.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Finger Puppets

Finger puppets are fun! They are available from craft, discount or toy stores or you can simply make your own. You and your child can create characters together and then have ‘conversations’ with them or sing songs.

This is a great way to introduce social skills such as using ‘please’ and ‘thank you’.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Playdough Characters

A fun playdough activity is to make characters together from a favourite book. You could both make the characters and talk about what they did, where they went and what they looked like.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Talk, Listen and Connect

Make your child feel special and important by showing interest in what they are doing, having eye contact and encouraging them in positive ways. Bend down and show that you are present and attending to what they are saying, even if you are in a rush. This ensures your child knows they are loved.

Talking and listening to your child from a young age can lay the foundations for an open relationship where children feel comfortable talking about what they have been doing and with whom.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Set Routines

Developing routines helps a child feel secure and builds predictive skills. You may like to begin talking over breakfast about what day it is and what you do on that particular day or the sequencing of events for that day.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Repetition and Context

Help your toddler learn new words by repeating them in different contexts. For example if the child is playing with a toy car you could say “You’ve got a car, it’s a green car. We have a red car. The car goes brrrmmmmm, it’s a fast car!”

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Helping Out

Encourage your child to help around the house with everyday household chores and gardening. Ask your child to follow directions such as sorting clothes, getting a dust pan and brush or carrying a bucket.

This develops listening skills as well as provides experiences to share with other family members. It also helps children develop a sense of responsibility.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Sing Songs

Have fun with songs. Singing songs with your child can help them to learn new words and sounds, develop their listening skills and lay the foundations for early literacy success.

Try nursery rhyme CDs in the car. You can both sing along together. You can also borrow CDs from your local library. Try songs with actions. Children this age love action songs and they help build their co-ordination skills.

View the Kindy Song Book

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Share Books

Spend time sharing books with your toddler. Try books with thick cardboard pages and bright colourful pictures. You can use the pictures to start a discussion and begin conversation about what else might be happening.

Reading a book together does not have to be inside. Why not read in the park, the garden or at the beach? Just remember to make reading experiences enjoyable so that your child learns to love books.

It is never too late to start reading to your child.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Felt Boards

Felt boards provide an interesting tactile experience for your child. They are available from book shops, toy stores or you can even have fun making your own from felt. Use the felt characters to act out stories. Encourage expression and use different voices for different characters.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Fabulous Junk Mail

Junk mail catalogues provide great, inexpensive material for sharing and talking together. You can say the name of the items in the photos.

The colourful images and photos of babies, young children and families are attractive to young children. Together you can make a book of favourite images from various catalogues and it is all free!

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Counting

Learning to recognise numbers can start by simply counting fingers and toes. Count out quantities of items frequently so that your child can hear the sequencing of numbers. Count simple things like the groceries, clothes pegs, rocks, shells and steps.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Questions and Comments

Be mindful of the amount of questions you ask your child. Too many questions make it seem like a test. When reading together, try to make comments as well as asking questions.

Encourage discussion, such as ‘What is it?’ ‘Where is it?’ ‘When did it happen?’ ‘Why did it happen?’ ‘How do you know that?’

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Positional Language

Your child can start learning different spatial concepts such as high/low, before/after, up/down, in front and behind through playing with toys, reading books, saying rhymes and singing songs. There are many useful books available at your local library.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Threading

Threading is a great way to practise counting and pattern recognition skills. You can use pasta such as penne or rigatoni. Try painting it different colours too. Your child will love making necklaces or wind ornaments with you.

Remember to be watchful when using small items around children to avoid choking.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Signs and Symbols

Explain signs and symbols when you see them. Over time a child will attach the correct meaning. For example, young children become aware that a red octagon with white writing is a stop sign and that it carries meaning to drivers.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Container Discovery

Many children love playing and stacking various size containers. When your child is engaged in this use it as an opportunity to introduce, repeat and model words such as up/down, in/out, more/less. Hours of fun can be spent with simple household items.

Toys do not need to be expensive. You can easily use everyday items to encourage and foster language development, curiosity and creativity.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Texture Time

Toddlers love different textures! Water play with different sized cups, playing in the sand pit, ball-pits, craft activities with wool, pipe cleaners, cottonwool and playdough all provide experiences which encourage talk.

When playing with different textures use descriptive words that relate to that object. For example, play dough is squishy, cotton-wool is fluffy, shaving cream is slimy. Why not take photos of these experiences so your child can share their fun with others at a later time?

Talking about the feel of things develops vocabulary. This is an important part of becoming a successful reader.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Moon Sand

Try making ‘moon sand’ at home. It is easy and inexpensive. The recipe is simply 4 cups sand, 2 cups cornflour and 1 cup of water. You can add food colouring or glitter to make it more exciting.

Go to the website link below and enter ‘moon sand’ for instructions and photos.

http://www.playbasedlearning.com.au

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Toy Time

Your child will begin to use teddies and dolls to represent real people in their play. They will act out everyday events which will give you a great opportunity to talk to them about real life situations.

An example of this might be ‘now that teddy has finished eating, he needs to clean his teeth’.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Puzzles

Play with puzzles together. Choose puzzles that have an appropriate number of pieces for your child’s age. 3D puzzles can be difficult for toddlers.

Talk about moving the pieces this way and that way until it fits into the appropriate place. Talk about edges, straight and curved sides and count the number of pieces together.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Play Sequencing

At this age children start to join two play sequences together. Your child’s attention span is being extended and he/she is demonstrating an understanding of their world around them as they recreate their experiences e.g. bathing the doll and then putting it to bed.

Talk with your child about these experiences. Remember children copy what they see so be a great role model for your child.

Word Count

By about 3 years of age most children use 3 to 4 word sentences and will use up to 300 words.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Listen Up!

When your child is trying to talk to you it is helpful if you can make eye contact at their level. Your little one knows they have your attention and will try to communicate with you. You are also demonstrating excellent listening skills.

Listening is important for your child’s development because it helps to teach the skills needed to read, communicate and build social skills.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Model Correct Language

If children say words that are not clear try to repeat what they have said using the right sounds e.g. if they say ‘tat’ you say ‘Yes, that’s a cat’. This is an effective way to help them to learn to speak clearly.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Growing Words

Repeat and expand upon what your child has said. For example, if they say ‘me want more milk’ you could say ‘Oh, you’d like some more milk in your cup?’

You could also describe objects in ways to extend their language e.g. ‘Look at the little brown dog.’

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

It Takes Time

Research does not support teaching young children things that their brains are not developmentally ready for.

Children this age may start to have some awareness of sounds and may begin to recognise their own name.

While they are not ready for kindergarten or school level activities, by encouraging their efforts, you are helping them learn important foundation skills.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Develop New Words

3 year olds can understand most sentences. Most of their words will be understandable to most people. Use new words to help build your child’s vocabulary e.g. this apple is delicious/scrumptious/crunchy etc.

Children with a larger vocabulary are better equipped to learn to read and comprehend what has been read to them.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

The Right Way

Your child may begin to use plurals (such as apples) and pronouns such as I, we, he, she, his and her. Make sure you model the correct use.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

One Thing at a Time

Did you know that most children struggle with concentrating on doing something while listening to other instructions? Your child needs the opportunity to stop what they are doing to process the instruction before they can proceed. This will allow for success and develop their self-esteem and confidence.

Back

13 months - 2.5 years

Whole Body Listening

Children need to be taught how to be good listeners. Encourage your child to listen with their whole body.

Listen with your ears, listen with your eyes by looking at the speaker, listen with your mouth by not talking and listen with your hands and legs by keeping them still.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Caring Relationships

The best learning happens in caring relationships. It is important to spend quality time with your child regardless of how busy you are.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Talk, Talk, Talk

Talking with young children helps them to develop speech, spoken and later written language skills. Talk to your toddler about what you are doing at the particular time and what you are doing next. Make lots of comments so they can, over time, attach meaning to your words.

The tone of your voice is as important as the words you say. Connect the tone of your voice with your facial expression e.g. if you are communicating in a happy tone match this with a smile.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Read Every Day

Literacy is the most important factor in determining your child’s early school success. By reading and sharing books and talking together, you are helping your child develop these early foundation skills. Make sure you spend time sharing books together, every day.

Try to think of this time as a special time to connect rather than a task you must do. This will support their love of reading and strengthen the special bond between you both. Make it fun and let your child choose the books.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

New Experiences

Try to expose your child to different experiences, people, situations and environments. Mix things up and try new things that are appropriate for young children e.g. visit a local farm, go to a plant nursery, join a playgroup, ride on the train, visit the beach, check out the programs for children at your library.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Celebrate

Celebrate achievements whether big or small. This may be a new word or phrase, great behaviour, making something together and other milestones. You do not need to buy your child a present, you just need to communicate to your child that you recognise and value what they have done. Talk about how they made you feel.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Car Travel

If you have a car DVD player, try to limit using it. Your child does not need television for short car trips because this reduces the opportunities for spontaneous conversation.

Try using something that encourages talking or singing such as music, stories, photos or a treasured toy.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Library Visits

Make a habit of visiting the library regularly together. There are so many different types of children’s books there.

You may want to mix a couple of new books with your child’s old favourites. Allow your child to always choose books for themselves.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Daily Talk

At dinner or bedtime, talk to your child about their day e.g. What happened? Who was there? How did it make you feel?

This helps to build vocabulary, memory and story-telling skills. It also shows your child that you are interested in how they perceive their day.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Question Time

Your child will start asking you lots and lots of questions including why, when, who, what and how.

Many parents find their children this age ask why questions over and over. Keep in mind that questions are an important strategy for gathering information and you are helping them to make sense of their world, thinking about how things work, what happens and why etc.

It is OK not to know the answer to every question so the following website may provide insight for you and your child into how things work if you do not already know:

http://www.howstuffworks.com/

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Know your Child

Learning about your child’s interests will help you understand them better. Focus your comments and questions on what they are interested in or focussing on. They are more likely to listen, learn and engage with you when your language is relevant to what they are doing.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Tune In

Your child is likely to be distracted when there is lots of background noise and distractions. It is more difficult to ‘tune in’ when background noise, such as television, is competing for their attention. Set time limits for the television and avoid leaving it on throughout the day.

Be aware of the amount of competing and background noise when your child is trying to concentrate.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Choice is Empowering

Give your child the ability to make a choice between two alternatives. For example, they may want to pick out which pyjamas they wear or choose between an apple or a banana for a snack. This empowers your child as they feel as though they have some say in their lives.

Never offer alternatives that you would be unhappy with e.g. ‘Do you want to go to bed now or later?’

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Role Model Reading

Be a reading role model. Let your children see you enjoying reading different books, magazines, comics and newspapers. Help them to understand the power of language and how it helps you throughout the day, every day.

An example might be a shopping list. You need to write the name of each item and then take the list with you to the shops. Explain that the purpose is to help you remember what you need to get. You can mark off each item. You can also count the items to develop number awareness.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Reading Tips

When reading a book with your child, sit where they can see your face. This will support them to make connections between emotions expressed in the story and your facial expressions.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Name Awareness

Help your child make the connection between their name and themselves. Talk about their name and write their name on things they make such as their artwork. Point out the purpose of naming their things e.g. it helps to identify their belongings.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Helping at Home

Involve your child in chores around the house making sure they are age appropriate and with safety in mind. This makes them feel important and is a fun way to introduce concepts such as colour, numbers and sorting.

You could play a counting game with pegs while doing the laundry.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

New words

Try introducing an unusual or rare word when you are playing together, doing chores or sharing a meal. You may like to use a more common word to express the meaning and make a connection to the new word e.g. ‘I’m washing the clothes, I’m doing the laundry.’

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Rhyming Fun

Children love rhyming songs, stories and nursery rhymes and hearing these helps to develop early literacy skills. Have fun acting out the words with your child.

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Cooking

Cook simple recipes together with steps that your child can do. This makes them feel valued and important. Cooking also helps develop fine motor skills and early mathematical concepts such as quantity, measuring etc.

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Hidden Treasure

A basket full of treasure is a simple and fun idea that provides an opportunity to introduce new words and adjectives. Use a container and put 3 or 4 objects in it. Cover with a tea towel and ask your child to put their hand underneath the tea towel and see if they can guess what is in there.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Puppets

Puppets help promote imaginative play and encourage your child’s narrating skills. It is fun and easy to make puppets together out of brown paper bags or cardboard cut-outs glued onto ice block sticks. Draw on faces and add hair and clothes with scrap material, wool and other recycled materials.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Pretend Play

Provide opportunities for pretend play. Dressing up and role playing helps children learn empathy as they experiment with others’ roles and feeling within a safe environment.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Make Your Own Music

Your child will love making their own sounds by using everyday items around the house. Turned over saucepans, bowls and plastic containers are all fun to hit with a wooden or plastic spoon. Paper towel rolls can be used as a wind instrument.

Support your child to investigate the different sounds each make and use words that reflect these differences.

Back

2.5 years - 3.5 years

Playdough Adventures

Use playdough to provide opportunities for creative play. Show how different shapes can be turned into something else. Talk together about what you are making and try to introduce new or unusual words that are relevant to what you are doing to extend your child’s vocabulary.

Attention and Eye Contact

Make sure that you have your child’s attention before you speak, as they may be concentrating on something else and not hear you. Get down to eye level and use eye contact when talking to them.

This models the expected behaviour when being spoken to and lets them know you are listening.

Artwork

Your child’s artwork is a great discussion point. Let them know that you like and value it by asking questions that encourage an explanation. Display your child’s artwork around the house to show them you appreciate what they have done.

Art also encourages eye-hand co-ordination, fine motor development, concentration and a sense of pride; all of which assist in developing self-confidence.

Where Am I?

Using the satellite navigation on the phone choose a location and talk about it. Where could it be? What makes you think that? Have you been there? What can you recognise? Look for shapes in the bird’s eye view and discuss them.

If it is a known location talk about what the street view would look like, why do things look different from the air?

4.5 years - 6 years

Listening Adventures

Go on a listening walk with your child. Make a map of where you will go together before you leave. Stop along the way to talk about what you have heard. You could also record the sounds on your phone and ask your child to name the sounds on your return.

Introduce new words to describe the sounds to build your child’s vocabulary e.g. the birds were chirping in the trees, the flag was flapping in the wind and the waves were crashing on the shore.

4.5 years - 6 years

Direction Games

Extend your child’s memory by playing direction games. Give three step directions, ‘Go into the kitchen, jump up and down three times then sit on the floor’.

If your child cannot remember all three instructions then make it simpler by giving two directions until they are competent to achieve these.

4.5 years - 6 years

Keeping It Real

Use every day events to have meaningful conversations with your child such as when you are shopping, cooking or feeding the pets. When gardening or cooking you can ask your child to fill items to the top or make them half full.

By doing these things you are helping your child understand measurement.

4.5 years - 6 years

Sharing Books

Sharing books with your child will help them to learn new words, grow to love books and build early literacy skills. Reading with your child allows for books and characters to come alive!

Remember children are more likely to read when they see adults reading as well. Set a great example for your child.

4.5 years - 6 years

Developing Story-telling Skills

Talking with your children about lifting and carrying items with your child can help develop oral language around numeracy. When children are lifting and carrying things they will talk about things such as ’heavy and light’ and ’big and small’.

Through these experiences children notice that the biggest thing is not always the heaviest and the smallest thing is not always the lightest. Having lots of experience like this helps children understand about ’mass’ (how dense something is). Happy talking!

Cooking Builds Numeracy Skills

Cooking is a great way to spend time together while teaching your child the language of Mathematics e.g. ‘We need to cut the carrots three times 1, 2, 3’. ‘Should we put half the potato in the pot or would you like to put the whole potato in?’, ‘Can you pass me two eggs please?’

Placemats

Make your own placemat! Ask your child to create a picture on a piece of paper by drawing or cutting out and gluing pictures from junk mail or magazines. Let them have ownership of their picture and ask them to tell you about it.

Ask probing questions such as “I wonder where this little dog is going...” You can laminate or use clear contact to protect it when in use.

Shopping

Shopping is a great way to extend your child’s knowledge of size (small, medium, large), shape (round, long, short, square) and weight (light or heavy). Take time to let your child choose. It will make the experience fun while extending their language.

Descriptive Games

Play describing games with your child e.g. ‘Today we are having a yellow fruit for morning tea. It has a peel to take off. It starts with a “b” sound. It’s a… banana.’ Encourage your child to build pictures in their mind as the object is described. This is called visualisation and is important for good readers as it heightens motivation and enjoyment of reading and improves comprehension.

Guess Who

Play ‘guess who’. Simply ask your child to pretend they are someone or something else and you need to ask them yes/no questions. For example, ‘Is it living? Is it bigger than a shoe box?’, then swap.

This will give your child lots of practise problem solving through asking questions and develops listening skills essential for effective communication.

What am I?

Place 10 regular house hold items on a table. These could include a spoon, cup, potato peeler, pen, comb, toothbrush etc. Ask your child to secretly choose one of the items in their mind. They then give continuous clues about the item until you are able to guess it e.g. “I’m thinking of something that is long, it has small brush at one end. You use it to clean your teeth.”

You may need to prompt your child at first to assist with the types of clues that need to be given. Swap roles and let your child do the guessing as this game also encourages problem solving.

4.5 years - 6 years

Make It Match

Place a thick piece of folded card between you and your child to form a barrier. You both need to have exactly the same picture and at least 5 coloured counters. Give your child explicit instructions of where to place each counter on the picture e.g. ‘Put the yellow counter on top of the red chair, put a green counter on the cat.’

Once you have used all the counters remove the barrier and check to see that the pictures have the counters in the same places. Swap with your child. And let them give the instructions.
